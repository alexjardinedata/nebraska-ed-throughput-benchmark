The open question is how much headroom remains as Medicaid coverage losses push unscheduled demand toward Lincoln.

Testable business question: Does Bryan's ED network show measurably better throughput than its Lincoln competitor and Nebraska acute-care peers in public CMS data — and does that finding hold after controlling for volume class and facility type?

05

Three questions for internal data

Public data shows the benchmark; these need Epic — and two connect directly to Medicaid coverage-loss exposure
1

Quantify behavioral-health boarding

Public medians can't see the admitted-and-waiting population, and behavioral health is the chronically capacity-constrained line.

Needs: Epic ADT timestamps + psych bed census · Produces: the boarding-hours decomposition a capacity investment case requires.
2
Connects to Medicaid work-requirement exposure

Build a coverage-loss early-warning monitor

Projected disenrollment of 16,000–30,000 Nebraskans converts covered, scheduled care into uninsured, unscheduled ED visits — and Bryan is the region's catch basin.

Needs: registration/payer-mix data, weekly, against state disenrollment waves · Produces: a leading indicator, weeks ahead of the income statement.
3
Connects to Medicaid work-requirement exposure

Forecast census 7–14 days out

Predict census by service line to flex staffing proactively rather than premium-pay reactively. Structurally the same forecasting problem as athlete availability — constrained capacity, seasonal demand, individualized risk.

Needs: admissions time series + the coverage-loss signal from #2 · Produces: staffing-grid inputs; success = fewer premium-labor hours at equal or better throughput.

Stated limitations

  • OP_18b covers discharged ED patients only — boarding of admitted patients isn't captured.
  • 0.0% walkouts is CMS-reported and may round from a small true rate.
  • Public hospital-quality data lags ~12 months by design (OP_18b: Jul 2024–Jun 2025; OP_22: CY 2024) — which is exactly why these recommendations point to internal data.
  • Bryan East and West share one CMS certification number; campus-level analysis requires internal data.
